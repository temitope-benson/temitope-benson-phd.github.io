Mathematical and Biological Exploration of Centrosome Maturation

Skills: Fluorescence Microscopy, C. elegans Maintenance, Data Analysis, Incorporating Data in Models, Scientific Writing & Presentations, LATEX, XMGrace, Inkscape
Programming: MATLAB, ODE/PDE Models, Parallel Computing Toolbox

Collecting in vivo data of the recruitment and recovery of a GFP tagged centrosomal protein in the early C. elegans embryo, I developed and parametrized a mathematical model of centrosome maturation. Analysis of a large set of parameters that fit our model to the in vivo data reveal that there are potential differences in the dynamics between the two centrosomes. The multi-scale microscopy and mathematical modeling together support our hypothesis that there is a previously uncharacterized asymmetry inside of the C. elegans centrosome that could be connected to cell fate decisions.

Turing Model of Crossover Positioning

Skills: Iterative Model Design, Matlab, Scientific Writing, Multi-lab Collaboration

During meiosis crossover of homologous chromosomes is required for proper development. The positioning of crossovers in C. elegans is a one-and-only-one process meaning a crossover will happen near either but not on the ends of the SC. This process has been previously hypothesized to follow the dynamics of Turing reaction-diffusion models given the observed crossover assurance and interference. I set out to recapitulate the patterning of crossovers with a reaction diffusion model. This shows that there could exist a mechanism that mimics the patterning of the Turing reaction-diffusion model to position a single crossover on the arms of the SC. Further, our model indicates that such a mechanism would rely strongly on the initial DSB pattern.

Agent-Based Model of Microcalcification Growth in Simulated Tissue

Skills: JAVA, Agent-Based Model, Image Analysis

When screening for breast cancer, the radiological interpretation of mammograms is a difficult task, particularly when classifying precancerous growth such as microcalcifications (MCs). We found that the fractal dimension of MCs changes with breast tissue density, which suggests tissue environment plays a role in regulating MC growth. Benign and malignant MCs are distinguishable in all types of tissue by shape, size, and area, which is consistent with findings in the literature.
These results may help to better understand the effects of the tissue environment on tumor progression, and improve classification of MCs in mammograms via computer-aided diagnosis.

Mathematical Modelling of Pollen Aperture Formation

Skills: FLEXPDE, MATLAB, Reaction-Diffusion, Collaboration with Biologists, Data Visualization

This work demonstrates the ability of the theoretical model to help focus experimental efforts and to provide fundamental insights into an important biological process. Pollen provides an excellent system to study pattern formation at the single-cell level. To explore possible mechanisms responsible for aperture patterning, we developed a mathematical model based on the Gierer-Meinhardt system of equations. This model was able to recapitulate aperture patterns observed in the wild-type and higher-ploidy pollen.

PhD Dissertation: Combining Biological Experimentation with Precise Quantification and Mathematical Modeling

Skills:Yes

The complexity of biological systems often defies straightforward understanding due to the multitude of interacting components and intricate feedback loops. Mathematical models can serve as abstract representations of real-world phenomena, capturing essential features while stripping away extraneous details. By formulating biological processes in mathematical terms, I gain the ability to simulate, manipulate, and explore the behavior of these systems under various conditions. Reaction-diffusion models and kinetic models are used to build a better understanding of otherwise difficult or impossible to determine biological processes. Combining novel mathematical modeling and a precise quantification of biological processes can uncover previously unseen mechanisms.

In this dissertation I explore three different systems, C. elegans centrosome maturation, C. elegans crossovers during meiosis, and Hydra vulgaris cell aggregate regeneration. In all of these models I iterate from a simple explanation of the biological system by adding new dynamics and complexity just until our models are able to recapitulate the data. This ethos allows for relatively simple models where all of the dynamics have a strong biological understanding. By taking an unbiased approach to experimental design and analysis I was able to simulate biological processes with many different types of models.
